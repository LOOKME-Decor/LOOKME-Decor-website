01 — The Workshop

Craft, made accountable.

Every commission moves through the same five checkpoints before it's signed off — a discipline borrowed from manufacturing, applied to furniture that's still built by hand.

1

Source

Timber selected and graded for grain, moisture and strength.

2

Draft

Measurements and joinery mapped before a single cut is made.

3

Shape

Cutting, planing and framing to the drawn specification.

4

Join

Joinery, assembly and hardware fitted for load and daily use.

5

Inspect

Final check against spec — only then does it earn the stamp.

Look Me Art, Décor & Industries builds furniture the way a workshop should — with a drawing before a cut, and an inspection before a delivery.

The company sits at the crossing of design thinking and industrial production: pieces are conceived with an eye for form, then manufactured with the discipline of a factory floor, so quality doesn't depend on which day a piece was made.

Alongside commercial furniture manufacturing, we run a skills training program — teaching practical trades, starting with furniture making, to students who want a craft they can earn from. It's covered in more detail below.

Whether you're furnishing a home, an office, or an entire hostel block, the process is the same: draft it properly, build it right, inspect it before it leaves.

03 — Skills & Enterprise

Teaching the trade, funding the beds.

We're developing a social enterprise program with a university partner: students are trained in practical trades — starting with furniture making — and the furniture the program produces, including hostel bed frames, is sold back to fund the training itself.

It's built to run in conversation with the university's proprietor and Vice-Chancellor's office, so training capacity and furniture output grow together rather than competing for resources.

1
Trade taught first: furniture making
2-in-1
Model: train students, supply hostels
Partner With Us
How the loop works
  1. Students enrol and train in furniture-making fundamentals on campus.
  2. Supervised production runs turn training hours into finished bed frames.
  3. Frames are sold to the university for hostel use at an institutional rate.
  4. Proceeds go back into the program — tools, materials and instructor time.
